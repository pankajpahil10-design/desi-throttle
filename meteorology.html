Aviation Meteorology — CPL/ATPL Depth

Aligned to: DGCA CAR Section 7, Series B, Part IV (Appendix B) + ICAO knowledge framework. Built for exam performance and operational decision quality.

1) Atmosphere, Thermodynamics, and Stability

Operational meteorology starts with energy behavior in air masses. For flight planning and risk judgment, pilots must understand lapse rates and static stability, not just cloud names.

Core relationships:
Pressure decreases with altitude; density depends on pressure and temperature.
ISA baseline at MSL: 15°C, 1013.25 hPa.
Approximate ISA lapse in troposphere: ~2°C / 1000 ft.

Stability logic: Compare environmental lapse rate with adiabatic lapse behavior. Stable profiles suppress vertical development (layer cloud bias); unstable profiles support vertical cloud growth, convection, and turbulence risk.

2) Pressure Systems and Wind Mechanics

Wind is governed by pressure-gradient force, Coriolis deflection, and friction. Aloft, near-balance states produce near-isobaric flow; near surface, friction drives cross-isobar inflow toward low pressure.

  • Geostrophic tendency: pressure gradient balanced by Coriolis (idealized, frictionless).
  • Surface wind: lower speed, greater isobar crossing angle, stronger convergence near lows.
  • Operational output: crosswind planning, drift correction, and frontal passage interpretation.

3) Cloud/Fog Physics and Visibility Risk

Cloud/fog formation is a thermodynamic lifting-cooling process to saturation. Distinguish mechanisms because each implies different persistence and runway impact.

  • Radiation fog: nocturnal cooling over land, weak wind regime.
  • Advection fog: warm moist air over colder surface, often persistent.
  • Frontal/upslope processes: may degrade alternates and increase holding/approach uncertainty.

4) Fronts, Convection, and Severe Hazards

Frontal geometry controls uplift style and hazard profile. Cold-front dynamics tend toward abrupt uplift and convective risk; warm-front structure often drives broader stratiform cloud and prolonged reduced ceiling/visibility.

  • CB / TS hazards: severe turbulence, hail, lightning, wind shear, microburst.
  • LLWS/microburst: critical at takeoff/landing; performance margins can collapse rapidly.
  • Pilot action: strategic avoidance and conservative alternate/fuel decisions.

5) Icing, Turbulence, Jet Streams

Icing and turbulence are performance and controllability threats. Risk is state-dependent: moisture phase, droplet size regime, temperature band, and flow structure all matter.

  • Icing envelope (operational): visible moisture + sub-zero band, with largest risk in supercooled liquid regimes.
  • Turbulence classes: mechanical, convective, clear-air (jet associated).
  • Jet stream relevance: time/fuel optimization vs CAT exposure management.

6) Altimetry and Non-Standard Atmosphere Errors

Altimetry error discipline is non-negotiable in instrument operations. Pressure setting and thermal deviation both alter terrain/obstacle margins.

Remember: In colder-than-ISA conditions, true altitude is lower than indicated for a given pressure surface (risk of reduced clearance).
  • QNH: MSL-referenced indication on ground.
  • QFE: airfield-reference zeroing concept.
  • QNE: 1013.25 hPa for flight levels.

7) METAR/TAF/SIGMET — Decision Use

Exam success requires decoding. Professional performance requires decision integration: trend awareness, confidence limits, alternate suitability, and contingency fuel implications.

  • Parse weather groups in operational sequence.
  • Track trend/probability groups for deterioration windows.
  • Convert decoded weather into dispatch/route/approach risk controls.

8) DGCA Exam Layer (How to score + retain)

  • Concept-first memory: do not memorize isolated facts without mechanism.
  • Error-log method: classify mistakes into physics, interpretation, or procedural logic.
  • Scenario drills: convert each concept into a flight-decision context question.
